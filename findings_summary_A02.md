# Assignment #02 — Telco Monthly Charge Prediction
## Findings Summary

**EECE 6544 · Summer 2026**  
**Dataset:** telco.csv — 7,043 customers, 10 service indicator columns  
**Model:** Multivariable Linear Regression  

---

## Q1. What does the base plan cost before any add-ons?

The intercept came out to **$24.97/mo**. That's what the model expects a
customer to pay if they have none of the 10 tracked services essentially
the floor price of just being a Telco customer.

---

## Q2. How much does each add-on contribute to the monthly bill?

| Service | Monthly Contribution |
|---------|---------------------|
| InternetService_Fiber optic | +$24.95 |
| PhoneService | +$20.04 |
| StreamingTV | +$9.98 |
| StreamingMovies | +$9.95 |
| OnlineSecurity | +$5.05 |
| TechSupport | +$5.03 |
| MultipleLines | +$5.01 |
| DeviceProtection | +$5.01 |
| OnlineBackup | +$4.98 |
| InternetService_No | -$25.05 |

The negative value for `InternetService_No` makes sense customers with
no internet at all skip every internet-based add-on, so their bill is
pulled down significantly from the average.

---

## Q3. Most expensive and cheapest add-on?

- **Most expensive:** Fiber optic internet (+$24.95/mo)
- **Cheapest / lowest:** InternetService_No (-$25.05/mo — it's a
  negative coefficient, not a real add-on cost)
- **Cheapest actual add-on:** OnlineBackup (+$4.98/mo)

Fiber is the biggest driver of monthly charges by far, which is what
you'd expect as it's a premium tier upgrade versus DSL.

---

## Q4. Fiber internet + both streaming services — how much does the bill rise?

A customer who adds fiber optic, StreamingTV, and StreamingMovies would
be predicted to pay **$69.85/mo**, which is **$44.88 above the base plan**.
That's the fiber premium ($24.95) plus the two streaming services
(~$9.98 + $9.95).

---

## Q5. Phone service + multiple lines + tech support — expected charge?

Predicted monthly charge: **$55.05/mo**  
That's the base ($24.97) + PhoneService ($20.04) + MultipleLines ($5.01)
+ TechSupport ($5.03).

---

## Q6. Is knowing *which* services better than knowing *how many*?

Yes, by a lot.

| Model | R2 | MAE | RMSE |
|-------|----|-----|------|
| Single-variable (count of add-ons) | 0.599 | $16.97 | $19.06 |
| Multivariable (each service) | **0.999** | **$0.79** | **$1.05** |

The count-based model is off by ~$17 on average because it treats every
add-on as equally priced. The multivariable model learns the actual price
of each service and is almost exact.

---

## Q7. How accurate are predictions on unseen customers?

- **R2 = 0.999** — the model explains 99.9% of the variance in monthly charges
- **MAE = $0.79** — on average, predictions are less than a dollar off
- **RMSE = $1.05** — even the larger errors are only about a dollar

The model is extremely accurate because Telco's pricing is essentially
deterministic — the monthly charge is a direct sum of service prices with
very little noise. This is an ideal use case for linear regression.

---

## Q8. Do the learned prices match Telco's actual price list?

Mostly yes, and the pattern is clean:
- Phone service: ~$20 — in line with typical base phone plan pricing
- Streaming TV and Movies: ~$10 each — consistent with standard streaming tiers
- Security, backup, protection, tech support: all clustered tightly around $5
- Fiber optic: ~$25 premium over DSL — expected for a higher-tier internet service

The one surprising coefficient is `InternetService_No` at -$25. It's not
really a price reduction — it's the model adjusting for the fact that
customers without internet have structurally lower bills because they can't
subscribe to any internet-dependent services. The model is technically
correct even if it looks odd at first glance.
